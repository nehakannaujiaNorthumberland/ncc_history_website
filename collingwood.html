Cutworth cullingwood

Admiral Cuthbert Collingwood, born in Newcastle upon Tyne, 1748, was the eldest son of a merchant, receiving a formal education in the Royal Grammar school, Newcastle. Early in life, Collingwood began his tenure within the royal navy, at only 12 years old, Collingwood took service under the command of his maternal cousin- Richard Braithwaite, aboard the frigate Shannon. While serving, Collingwood displayed exemplary tactical insight, partaking in battles such as the Glorious first of June and the Battle of Cape St Vincent in 1797. Collingwood’s most widely renowned victory is largely considered to be the success he saw during the Battle of Trafalgar, 1805, a battle that acted to crown the British navy as an unrivalled force around the globe- Collingwood saw a decisive victory against the Franco-Spanish fleets after Admiral Nelson fell in the advent of the battle.

Collingwood’s Love of Morpeth

His clear affinity for sea life and honourable dedication to his profession is best expressed when considering his 49 years of service to the navy, only 4 of which he spent on land; even taking his last breaths on the rolling waves aboard the HMS Ville de Paris in 1810. Despite this, Collingwood still held great affections for his land- bound Morpeth manor, Collingwood house, where his wife, Sarah Blackett; himself, and his 2 daughters took residency. Northumberland and Morpeth had a particularly moving effect on him despite the little time he spent on land- to this day his Oldgate home bears both his name and his affections for Morpeth, the placard visible atop Collingwood house reading “whenever I think how I am to be happy again, my thoughts carry me back to Morpeth."

Battle of Trafalgar

The Battle of Trafalgar, taking place 21 October 1805, was a major naval engagement of the Napoleonic wars in which the British Royal Navy saw resounding success over the efforts of Spanish-Franco fleets. The overwhelming victory of Britain played a vital role in preventing Napoleon’s invasion of England by maintaining British control of the English channel. Leading the British fleets, Admiral Horatio Nelson, along with Admiral Collingwood as his right hand man, had the plight of taking on the combined force of the Spanish and French, outnumbered 27 to 33. Despite these poor odds, the careful planning of the battle led to a show of tactical triumph- the British fleets were divided into two squadrons, Nelson taking command of 12 ships, leading them towards the enemy flagship while Collingwood, aboard the HMS Royal Sovereign, oversaw 15 ships which were tasked with taking up the rear of the enemy line. During the battle, Nelson suffered a gunshot wound ultimately causing his death- Collingwood adopted charge of the fleets; as a result of prior strategic planning, superior gun capabilities and the vastly more experienced sailors, the British reigned victorious. Trafalgar was a sweeping success, only 1,500 British seamen were killed, as opposed to the enemy fleets suffering approximately 14,000 losses. For Collingwood’s performance, he was awarded a Baron title and granted a pension of £2,000 a year. The Battle of Trafalgar acted to reinforce the Royal Navy's invincibility- beginning the era of the British Navy's complete dominance in naval force.

Why was the Battle of Trafalgar important

The emphatic nature of Nelson’s victory at Trafalgar was important. It has been said that, while other British admirals may have also prevailed in the battle, Nelson did so in such a style that he effectively sealed Britain’s reputation as the world’s leading naval power for more than a century.

Collingwood’s love of his Dog - Bounce

Collingwood’s most dear companion both on land and on foot was his dog, Bounce. At his heel for a cherished 19 years- Collingwood recounts his dog bounce as a “charming creature” in letters to his sister; a constant delight to have aboard his ship. His fondness for his old dog was a consistent theme within his letters to both his family and more official documents, from time to time bounce’s name would make an appearance in postscripts, sending well wishes to the pets of the recipients.

Collingwood’s Relationship with Nelson

In 1777, Aboard the HMS Lowestoffe, Collingwood met Horatio Nelson- sparking the beginning of a lifelong friendship. Collingwood, despite being 10 years Nelson's senior, held no feeling of contempt or competition towards the younger, even as Nelson progressed in his naval career at enviable speeds. Reflective of his character; Collingwood good-naturedly praised Nelson: “Lord Nelson is an incomparable man, a blessing to any country…His successes in most of his undertakings are the best proofs of his genius”, Collingwood wrote to Dr Alexander Carlyle (August 1801).

Collingwood’s Improvements made to Royal Navy

Caring for his crew

Collingwood maintained a standard of generosity and fairness towards all the sailors that he commanded- he was known to be opposed to cruel methods of punishment such as flogging. This standard of morality he upheld gained him the nickname “father” by common sailors. Sailor Robert Hay wrote in his memoir about Collingwood, describing how Collingwood and “his favourite dog bounce were well known to every member of the crew”, Collingwood being acknowledged as "a better seaman, a better friend to seamen, a greater lover and a more zealous defender of the country's rights and honour, never trod the quarterdeck."

Improving Cannon Firing rate

Advised by a neighbour Collingwood delivered improvements that enabled Royal Navy to fire 3 times the rate of other navies.

Where is Collingwood buried

In 1810, Collingwood died of cancer on board HMS Ville de Paris as he sailed for England. He was laid to rest beside Lord Nelson in St Paul's Cathedral.
